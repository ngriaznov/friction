## BGP Route Flapping Between Upstream ISPs: Diagnosis and Troubleshooting

**Problem:** You have a multihomed router connected to two upstream ISPs via BGP peering sessions.  You're experiencing frequent route flapping, where routes are constantly being advertised and withdrawn between the two ISPs. This can lead to instability, performance issues, and routing loops. 

**Diagnosis:**

1. **Analyze Router Logs:** Start by examining your router logs for any error messages or warnings related to BGP updates. Look for:
    * **`BGP update message received with invalid attributes`:** Could indicate a malformed update from one of the ISPs.
    * **`Route flap detected on peer [AS number]`:** This explicitly flags route flapping. The router will usually log the affected routes and timestamps.

2. **Review BGP Statistics:** Access your router's BGP configuration and examine the "flapd" statistics:

   - `show ip bgp [AS number] neighbors`: Displays information about each BGP neighbor, including recent flap count (`neighbor_flaps`), route refresh rate, and other relevant metrics.
   - `show ip bgp summary`: Provides a high-level overview of BGP sessions, including the total number of flaps detected across all neighbors.

3. **Identify Affected Routes:** Analyze which routes are flapping most frequently. This can help pinpoint specific prefixes or network blocks causing instability.

**Understanding Route Dampening:**

Route dampening is a technique that mitigates the effects of route flapping by temporarily suppressing updates for routes experiencing frequent changes. While it can reduce the immediate impact, it's crucial to remember:

* **Dampening Masks Real Problems:**  While effective in managing symptoms, dampening doesn't address the root cause of the issue. Continuous flapping suggests underlying problems that need investigation and resolution.
* **Choose Dampening Parameters Carefully:** Overly aggressive dampening can lead to legitimate routes being suppressed, potentially causing routing disruptions.

**Tracing the Flapping Peer Link:**

1. **Analyze BGP Connection Logs:** Examine logs for each ISP peer connection. Look for timestamps correlating with route flaps and identify any patterns or inconsistencies in update frequencies between the peers.
2. **Utilize Network Monitoring Tools:** Employ network monitoring tools like:
    * **`traceroute`**: To trace the path of BGP updates between your router and each ISP peer. Identify potential bottlenecks or issues along the path.
    * **`ping`**: To check for connectivity problems between your router and each ISP peer.

3. **Contact Your ISPs:** Collaborate with your ISPs to gather insights into their network infrastructure and configurations. They may have information about recent changes or events affecting BGP routing stability.


**Remember:** 

- Document all findings meticulously, including timestamps, affected routes, and error messages.
-  Isolate the issue by temporarily disabling one ISP connection at a time to observe if flapping persists.
- Apply best practices for BGP configuration, such as using unique AS numbers, robust filtering rules, and proper neighbor relationships.


By following these steps and collaborating effectively with your ISPs, you can diagnose the root cause of BGP route flapping and implement solutions to ensure stable and reliable routing between your network and upstream providers.
