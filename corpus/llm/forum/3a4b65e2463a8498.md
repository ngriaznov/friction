**Title: Planning My Homelab NAS Build – Why I Chose RAIDZ2 Over Mirrored Vdevs**

Hello everyone,

I’m excited to share my recent journey in planning a homelab NAS build, and I wanted to outline the decisions that guided me toward choosing a RAIDZ2 vdev layout with 8 drives. This decision process involved careful consideration of performance, redundancy, and space efficiency, especially given my specific drive model’s characteristics.

### Choosing RAIDZ2 Over Mirrored Vdevs

Firstly, let’s talk about why I opted for RAIDZ2 (also known as ZFS RAID-6) over mirrored vdevs. The primary reason is the enhanced data protection it offers. With a mirrored setup (RAID-1), you only need one drive to fail before you lose access to your data. RAIDZ2, on the other hand, can withstand two simultaneous drive failures without data loss—a critical factor for me given my homelab’s environment.

**Performance Considerations**

Another key aspect was performance. RAIDZ2 provides a good balance between speed and redundancy. While mirrored setups are slower due to their write amplification (since each write operation requires writing to both drives), RAIDZ2 allows for more efficient use of space, which translates into better read/write speeds. This is especially important in my homelab where I anticipate heavy workloads from media transcoding and large file transfers.

**Space Efficiency**

RAIDZ2 also offers superior space efficiency compared to mirrored setups. With 8 drives configured in RAIDZ2, you get the capacity of 6 usable drives (since two are reserved for parity), which is a significant advantage over mirroring where all drives contribute equally to storage capacity. This means I can store more data without having to invest in additional hardware.

### Selecting the ashift Value

Choosing the right `ashift` value was crucial, especially since my drive model plays a role here. The `ashift` parameter determines how ZFS allocates space at the block level on the physical media. It’s essential for optimizing performance and ensuring compatibility with your specific hardware.

For my build, I selected an `ashift` of 12, which is suitable for most modern hard drives that use a 512-byte sector size. This value ensures that ZFS can efficiently map logical blocks to physical sectors without wasting space due to alignment issues. If the drive model had used a different sector size (e.g., 4KB), I would have adjusted `ashift` accordingly, likely setting it to 14 for optimal performance.

### What I’d Do Differently With Hindsight

Looking back, there are a few adjustments I’d make based on my experience and newfound knowledge:

1. **Drive Selection**: Initially, I chose drives primarily based on price and capacity without fully considering their reliability and speed characteristics. If I could go back, I would prioritize enterprise-grade SSDs or high-end HDDs known for durability and performance consistency.

2. **RAID Configuration Depth**: While RAIDZ2 is robust, having a deeper understanding of the implications of drive failure scenarios earlier on might have led me to consider adding more drives initially to increase redundancy further. This could be mitigated by setting up additional vdevs or using a combination of RAID levels for different data types.

3. **Monitoring and Maintenance**: I underestimated how crucial regular monitoring and maintenance would be, especially with the increased complexity of RAIDZ2 setups. Implementing automated health checks and backups from day one would have been beneficial in ensuring long-term reliability.

### Conclusion

In summary, my choice to build a homelab NAS using a RAIDZ2 vdev layout with 8 drives was driven by a need for robust data protection, performance efficiency, and optimal space utilization. The careful selection of the `ashift` value ensured that I leveraged the full potential of my drive model without compromising on speed or reliability.
