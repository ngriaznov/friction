When you’re setting up a custom long-running daemon using systemd on Linux, crafting the right `systemd` unit file is crucial for ensuring your service runs smoothly and reliably. Let’s break down some key components: `Restart=on-failure`, `WatchdogSec`, and `sd_notify` keepalives, along with understanding the differences between `Type=simple` and `Type=notify`.

First off, `Restart=on-failure` tells systemd to automatically restart your service if it crashes or stops unexpectedly. This is like a safety net—ensuring that even if something goes wrong during execution, your service will keep running in the background without manual intervention. To enable this, simply add:

```ini
Restart=on-failure
```

in your unit file.

Next, `WatchdogSec` is used to set a timeout for your service. If your daemon doesn’t send an update (keepalive) within this specified time, systemd will restart it as if there was a failure. This is particularly useful for daemons that might be momentarily unresponsive but are expected to stay alive most of the time. For example:

```ini
WatchdogSec=60
```

This means your service should send an update every 60 seconds; otherwise, it’ll trigger a restart.

Now, `sd_notify` keepalives are used for signaling systemd about the health status of your process without needing to use a full-fledged watchdog. When you emit notifications like `D+1` or `D-1`, it indicates that your service is alive (`+1`) or has crashed (`-1`). This keeps systemd informed without overloading it with unnecessary checks. Here’s how you might set this up:

```ini
ExecStart=/path/to/your/driver
NotifyStatus=waiting
ExecStartPost=/path/to/your/driver --start
NotifyReady=/path/to/your/driver --ready
```

When your service starts, it sends a `D+1` notification to let systemd know everything is operational. If something goes wrong and you need to inform systemd of a crash, sending `D-1` will trigger a restart.

Now, onto the difference between `Type=simple` and `Type=notify`. The default type for most services is `simple`, which means systemd waits for your service to start before it considers it running. Essentially, once you call your program in `ExecStart=` (like `/usr/bin/your-service`), systemd thinks everything’s fine.

On the other hand, `Type=notify` tells systemd that your process will send a notification when it’s ready. This is often used with services that have complex initialization steps or need to signal readiness before they’re considered fully operational by systemd. Using `NotifyStatus`, as mentioned earlier, allows you to communicate this readiness status directly.

Here’s a short example of what a complete unit file might look like for your custom daemon:

```ini
[Unit]
Description=Your Custom Long-Running Daemon
After=network.target

[Service]
Type=simple
User=daemonuser
Group=daemongroup
ExecStart=/usr/bin/your-service --start
Restart=on-failure
WatchdogSec=60
ExecStartPost=/usr/bin/your-service --ready
NotifyStatus=waiting
ExecStop=/usr/bin/your-service --stop

[Install]
WantedBy=multi-user.target
```

In this example, the daemon will restart automatically on failure and send a keepalive every 60 seconds. The `NotifyStatus` ensures systemd knows when it’s ready to handle requests.

By carefully configuring these options, you can ensure your custom long-running daemon remains reliable and responsive under varying conditions.
