**Subject: Diagnosing Persistent BGP Route Flapping Between Upstream ISPs – A Detailed Approach**

**Posted by:** NetworkGuru82 (Network Engineering)
**Date:** October 26, 2023
**Accepted Answer:** Yes

Okay, route flapping in BGP is a *nightmare*.  It’s incredibly frustrating and can lead to connectivity issues for your customers. When you see routes disappearing and reappearing frequently between two upstream ISPs (let's call them ISP-A and ISP-B), it demands immediate attention. Here’s a breakdown of how to systematically diagnose this issue:

**1. Initial Observation & Confirmation – Don't Panic!**

* **Verify the Flap:**  First, confirm that it *is* genuinely flapping. Don’t just assume you see anomalies. Use tools like `ping`, `traceroute`, and crucially, your BGP monitoring system (see below). Persistent packet loss is a key indicator.
* **Short-Term vs. Chronic:** Is this a brief occurrence or happening every few minutes?  A short, occasional flap might be transient network congestion, but chronic flapping points to a deeper problem.


**2. Leveraging BGP Monitoring – Your Primary Tool**

This is *essential*. You need granular data. Here's what you should be looking at:

* **BGP Router Advertisements (RA) Stats:** Most routers have counters for the number of RA announcements sent and received. A sudden drop in received RAs from a peer immediately indicates a problem.
* **BGP Route Flap Statistics:** This is *critical*.  Look for counters like “Route Received” vs. “Route Accepted”, “Route Changed”, "Route Deleted”. These will show you the frequency of updates, and whether your router is rapidly rejecting/accepting routes. The specific names vary by vendor (Cisco's are detailed, Juniper’s less so).
* **BGP Session State:**  Monitor the session state – is it “Established,” “Active,” or, alarmingly, "Idle" or “Holding”? Frequent transitions between these states signal instability.
* **BGP Neighbor Protocol Errors:** Look for error codes from your neighbors (e.g., TCP resets).

**3. Route Dampening - A Double-Edged Sword**

Route dampening is designed to mitigate flapping by reducing the advertisement of routes that aren’t being observed. *However*, it can mask a real underlying problem! 

* **When It Helps:** If you have genuine, intermittent network congestion between your router and an ISP, route dampening can temporarily stabilize things by reducing the frequency of updates.
* **When It Masks:**  If the flapping is caused by a persistent physical link issue (bad cable, faulty transceiver), or a misconfiguration on the ISP’s side, route dampening will simply *throttle* the problem, making it harder to diagnose. 

**4. Investigating the Root Cause – Traceback & Link Analysis**

This is where you move beyond just seeing the flapping and try to pinpoint the source:

* **BGP Route Tracebacks:** Use BGP route tracebacks (e.g., `show ip bgp <prefix> null-route` on Cisco) to see *where* the routes are originating from.  Does the flap start with a route advertised by ISP-A, then propagate through to ISP-B? This helps narrow down which peer is causing the issue.
* **Look at Path Attributes:** Examine the path attributes (AS_PATH, MED, etc.) of the flapping routes. Are there inconsistencies that might be triggering dampening?
* **Check Link Status:** The most important step!  Use your router’s interface status commands to check the physical and link layer status of *both* ISP connections. Look for errors like CRC failures, high collision counts, or dropped packets – these are strong indicators of a physical problem.
* **Ping/Traceroute (Carefully):** While pinging BGP peers directly is generally discouraged (it can interfere with BGP), use traceroute to see the path between your router and *both* ISPs.  Are there hops where latency or packet loss is consistently high?

**5. Collaboration – Talk to Your ISPs!**

Don’t waste time on this alone.  Work with ISP-A and ISP-B's network engineers. Share your monitoring data, let them know you're seeing flapping, and ask them to investigate their side of the connection. They may be experiencing issues that are impacting your routing.


**Example Scenario:**

Let’s say your BGP flaps show routes originating from ISP-A. You check the link status on your interface connected to ISP-A and see a recurring CRC error. This strongly suggests a faulty cable or transceiver is causing intermittent connectivity problems, triggering the flapping.  Route dampening isn't solving anything; it's just making the problem harder to spot.
