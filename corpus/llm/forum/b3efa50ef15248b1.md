## Project: Fortress - Building a Resilient Home NAS (RAIDZ2 & Lessons Learned)

**Posted by:** DataDrifter
**Date:** October 26, 2023
**Category:** General Discussion > Projects
**Replies:** 42


Okay, folks, I wanted to document my recent experience building out a home NAS – codenamed “Fortress” for dramatic effect. It’s been quite the journey of learning and occasionally, frustrating troubleshooting. The goal was simple: a reliable storage solution for backups, media streaming, and eventually some light home server applications like Plex.  I've spent a *lot* of time researching RAID configurations, and I wanted to share my thought process, specifically why I went with RAIDZ2 and the intricacies involved in setting it up.

**The Initial Plan & Why Not Mirroring?**

Right from the start, mirroring was screaming at me. It seemed so intuitive - double the redundancy! However, after a deep dive into the pros and cons, I realized mirrored RAID (RAID1) wasn't the best fit for my budget and long-term needs. With eight drives, building a mirrored array would require *eight* drives – a significant investment upfront.  Furthermore, mirroring has a 50% overhead in storage capacity, which is a huge waste when you’re looking to maximize usable space.

That's where RAIDZ2 came into the picture. It offered significantly more usable storage for the same number of drives (effectively using all eight) while still providing excellent redundancy. The core concept – a single parity drive protecting against both drive failures and, crucially, dual drive failures - was incredibly appealing.  I’d read countless articles recommending RAIDZ2 for home NAS builds, and the peace of mind it offered outweighed the slightly more complex initial setup.


**Choosing the Ashift Value: A Deep Dive with WD Red Pro 8TB**

Now, the ‘RAIDZ2’ part isn't just a label; you need to specify an 'ashift' value, which dictates the block size used within the RAID array. This is *absolutely* critical for performance and compatibility.  I opted for ashift=8 because I was using Western Digital Red Pro 8TB drives. WD explicitly recommends ashift=8 for their Red Pro series, and ignoring this would have led to severe performance issues – essentially crippling the entire array. The ashift value is directly tied to the drive’s internal sector size; an incorrect setting can cause massive overhead and dramatically slow down writes.  I spent a *lot* of time researching this aspect, referencing WD’s documentation and various online forums dedicated to Red Pro configurations.

**The Build & Initial Setup (and Early Mistakes!)**

The build itself went surprisingly smoothly. I used a Supermicro JB431T-8G NAS chassis – a solid piece of kit with plenty of cooling.  I installed the WD Red Pros, configured FreeNAS/TrueNAS Core initially (a good learning experience!), and then migrated to Unraid for its simplicity and flexibility.

Here's where things went sideways: I was *way* too focused on getting everything 'perfect’ in the initial setup. I spent an excessive amount of time meticulously configuring permissions, setting up user accounts, and scrutinizing every single drive status report. This led me to prematurely trigger a rebuild after a seemingly minor drive health check flagged a slight discrepancy. Panic ensued!  I immediately assumed the worst – a failing drive - and initiated a full rebuild, which took *forever*. 

**Hindsight & What I'd Do Differently:**

Looking back, that’s where my biggest regret lies. I should have embraced the fact that drives will show minor anomalies from time to time, especially during initial setup and after firmware updates. The drive was perfectly healthy; it was simply reporting a data difference related to its wear-leveling algorithm.  I realize now that aggressive monitoring can be detrimental – constantly checking for errors leads to unnecessary rebuilds and wasted time. 

Here’s what I would change:

* **Relax the Monitoring:**  Instead of obsessively monitoring drive health, I'd set up a system to automatically trigger rebuilds *only* when a critical error occurs (e.g., read/write errors exceeding a certain threshold).
* **Accept Minor Anomalies:** Learn to differentiate between genuine problems and normal wear-leveling behavior.  Research the specific anomalies reported by the drive – often, they are benign.
* **Test Before Panic:** If I saw something unusual, I would *first* attempt a data integrity check (like `mdadm`’s ‘check’) before jumping to conclusions and triggering a rebuild.
* **Invest in Better Cooling:** The Supermicro chassis was adequate, but the drives were running hot under sustained load. Adding some active cooling would have improved performance and potentially helped prevent thermal throttling.



**Current Status & Lessons Learned:**

Fortress is now operational and performing admirably. I’ve configured it for backing up my primary workstation, hosting a small Plex server, and storing media files. The RAIDZ2 setup provides robust redundancy, and I'm confident in its ability to withstand drive failures. 

The biggest takeaway from this project isn’t just the technical aspects of building a NAS; it’s the importance of patience, understanding drive behavior, and resisting the urge to immediately panic at every little warning light.  It was a fantastic learning experience – one that I'll definitely be using as I tackle future homelab projects!
