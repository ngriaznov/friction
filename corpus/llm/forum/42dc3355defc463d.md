**Title:** My Journey to Building an Efficient Homelab NAS - Choosing the Right RAIDZ2 Layout

**Posted by:** Zaphod3000

Hey fellow tech enthusiasts,

I'm excited to share my experience in planning and building a homelab NAS that meets my storage needs while also providing redundancy and data integrity. After months of research, I finally settled on a configuration that balances performance with reliability. In this post, I'll walk you through the reasoning behind choosing a RAIDZ2 vdev layout with 8 drives over mirrored vdevs.

**Why not Mirrored Vdevs?**

Initially, I considered using two mirrored vdevs (each with four drives) to achieve redundancy and simplicity. However, as I delved deeper into the world of ZFS, I realized that this approach would limit my storage capacity and not take full advantage of the drive count. With mirrored vdevs, you're essentially wasting half the available space due to the mirroring requirement.

RAIDZ2 (redundant array of independent disks level 2) offered a more efficient way to utilize all eight drives while still providing redundancy. By spreading the data across multiple drives and using parity information, RAIDZ2 ensures that even if one or two drives fail, the system can continue to operate with minimal data loss.

**Choosing the Right ashift Value**

Another crucial aspect of ZFS configuration is setting the ashift value, which determines how data is stored on disk. The ashift value should be set based on the native sector size of the drive. For my specific model, a 12TB Western Digital Caviar (WD122KFDX) with 4KB sectors, I chose an ashift value of 12.

Some sources suggest using ashift=9 for newer drives to take advantage of larger sector sizes and improve performance. However, I opted for the more conservative approach, considering my drive model's specifications and potential compatibility issues with future firmware updates or motherboard revisions.

**RAIDZ2 vdev Layout**

With my decision to use RAIDZ2 settled, I designed a layout that maximizes storage capacity while maintaining redundancy:

* 8 drives: Western Digital Caviar (WD122KFDX) - 12TB each
* Two separate vdevs with four drives each, using the "mirror" function within ZFS
	+ Each vdev would contain two RAIDZ2 vdevs, each comprising three disks, for a total of four RAIDZ2 vdevs

This configuration allows me to use all eight drives while still benefiting from redundancy. The two mirrored vdevs provide an additional layer of protection against a single drive failure within each mirror.

**What I'd Do Differently with Hindsight**

After finalizing my design, I couldn't help but think about potential improvements for future iterations:

* Consider using ashift=9 to take advantage of the newer drives' larger sector sizes
* Use a more aggressive configuration for the ZIL (ZFS Intent Log) and L2ARC (Level 2 Adaptive Replacement Cache)
	+ Increase the ZIL size or adjust its placement for better performance and reduced latency
	+ Experiment with larger cache sizes to improve read performance

**Conclusion**

Building an efficient homelab NAS requires careful consideration of various factors, including drive selection, RAID configuration, and ashift value. While it's easy to get caught up in the latest trends and technologies, I've learned that a well-planned approach often yields better results.
