Hello everyone, I'm excited to share my latest homelab project with you all - a NAS build using a RAIDZ2 configuration. As some of you may know, I've been planning this build for quite some time, and I'd like to walk you through the reasoning behind my choices.

**Choosing RAIDZ2 over Mirrored VDEVs**

When deciding on the storage layout for my homelab NAS, I initially considered using mirrored vdevs. However, after researching and weighing the pros and cons, I decided to go with a RAIDZ2 configuration instead. Here's why:

1. **Data redundancy**: RAIDZ2 provides an additional layer of redundancy compared to mirrored vdevs, which only offers 1:1 data duplication. With RAIDZ2, I can recover from a single drive failure without losing all my data.
2. **Performance**: While mirrored vdevs are great for simplicity and reliability, they can result in slower performance due to the added overhead of duplicate parity blocks. RAIDZ2, on the other hand, offers better performance by using a shared parity block.
3. **Flexibility**: With a RAIDZ2 layout, I have more flexibility when it comes to drive selection and replacement. If one of my drives fails, I can simply replace it without having to worry about rebuilding the entire vdev.

**8 Drives vs. 4/6 Drives**

When deciding on the number of drives for my RAIDZ2 setup, I opted for 8 drives instead of the more common 4 or 6 drive configurations. Here's why:

1. **Data capacity**: With 8 drives, I can store a larger amount of data, which is essential for a homelab NAS that will be used by multiple users.
2. **Redundancy**: Having more drives provides an additional layer of redundancy, making it even harder to lose all my data in case of a drive failure.

**Ashift Value**

When choosing the ashift value for my specific drive model, I considered several factors:

1. **Drive type**: My drives are 4KB block size, which is relatively small compared to newer models.
2. **Performance requirements**: Since my NAS will be used for general-purpose storage and sharing, I didn't need extremely high performance.
3. **Balance between performance and redundancy**: After researching and testing different ashift values, I decided on an ashift value of 4.

**What I'd Do Differently with Hindsight**

While I'm happy with my current setup, there are a few things I would do differently if I had the chance:

1. **Use a more robust drive model**: While my drives are reliable, I've noticed that some of them can be prone to vibration and noise issues. If I were to rebuild my NAS, I'd consider using more robust drive models that are better suited for homelab applications.
2. **Increase the number of redundancy zones**: Although my RAIDZ2 setup provides excellent redundancy, I would consider increasing the number of redundancy zones to further ensure data protection.
3. **Monitor and maintain my NAS regularly**: As with any homelab project, regular monitoring and maintenance are essential to prevent issues like drive failures or software corruption.

**Conclusion**

In conclusion, my homelab NAS build using a RAIDZ2 configuration has been a great success so far. By choosing this layout over mirrored vdevs, I've gained better redundancy, performance, and flexibility. While there's always room for improvement, I'm happy with the results and look forward to sharing more of my homelab adventures with you all in the future.
