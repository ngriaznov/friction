##  Homelab NAS Build - My RAIDZ2 Journey (with Hindsight!)

Hey everyone! So I finally took the plunge and built my own homelab NAS recently. It's been a learning experience, especially when it comes to RAID configurations. After much research, I decided on a RAIDZ2 vdev layout with 8 drives.  

Before I dive into the details, let me preface this by saying that there are always different approaches, and what works for one person might not be ideal for another. This is just my experience and what I learned along the way.

**Why RAIDZ2 over Mirrored Vdevs?**

Initially, I was leaning towards mirrored vdevs (RAID1). After all, it seemed like the safest option with double redundancy. But then I started digging deeper into data storage concepts and realized that mirroring isn't always the most efficient use of space. 

With RAIDZ2, you get the benefit of parity protection across three drives, ensuring data safety even if two drives fail simultaneously. This means I can lose up to two drives without losing any data, which is more than enough for my needs. Plus, RAIDZ2 offers a significant advantage over mirroring in terms of storage capacity utilization – with 8 drives, I get around 50% usable space compared to roughly 33% with mirrored vdevs.

**Choosing the `ashift` Value:**

One crucial aspect of setting up RAIDZ is determining the `ashift` value. This value essentially dictates how much data each drive can hold before needing to write to another drive. It's a balancing act between performance and redundancy.

My chosen drives are 18TB Seagate IronWolf Pro drives. After extensive research, I settled on an `ashift` value of 12 for these drives. This means that each drive can handle up to 256MB chunks of data before writing to another drive in the RAIDZ2 vdev.

Choosing the right `ashift` value is a bit of trial and error, depending on your specific hardware and workload.

**Hindsight: What I Would Do Differently:**

Looking back, there are a few things I would have done differently:

* **Start with a smaller setup:** While 8 drives seem like plenty now, I could have started with a smaller RAIDZ2 configuration (like 4 drives) to get my feet wet and understand how it all works. This would have been less intimidating initially.
* **Invest in a dedicated UPS:** Power outages can be devastating for a NAS. A reliable Uninterruptible Power Supply (UPS) is essential for protecting your data during unexpected power interruptions. I'm planning on getting one soon, but it's something I should have prioritized from the beginning.
* **Consider hardware RAID:** While software-based RAID solutions like ZFS are powerful and flexible, dedicated hardware RAID controllers can often offer better performance and reliability. This is something I'll explore for future NAS builds.

**Overall, Building My Own NAS Has Been a Great Experience:**

Despite a few bumps along the way, building my own homelab NAS has been incredibly rewarding. Learning about RAID configurations, optimizing storage settings, and customizing my setup has been a valuable learning experience.


I hope this post is helpful for anyone considering building their own NAS! Feel free to ask any questions you might have.
