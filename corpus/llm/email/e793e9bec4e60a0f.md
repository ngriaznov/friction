Subject: Finally – The Walnut Table is Done! (And a Lesson Learned!)

Hey Everyone,

Just wanted to share some incredibly exciting news - after what felt like an absolutely monumental effort, I’ve finally finished building my walnut dining table! It's been a long-term project, and seeing it sitting in the dining room feels absolutely incredible. I thought I’d give you all a rundown of the build, including a few bumps along the way – because let’s be honest, that’s half the fun (and learning experience) of woodworking!

The whole journey started with the wood selection, which was honestly one of my favorite parts. I spent weeks scouring local lumberyards and online suppliers looking for something truly special. Ultimately, I settled on a beautiful piece of Black Walnut from a small mill in Oregon – it’s about 6/4” thick, and has this gorgeous deep chocolate color with those amazing swirling grain patterns. The heartwood is incredibly dense and stable, which was really important for a table that will see heavy use. I chose a single board roughly 8/4" wide to give me good thickness for the legs and a substantial tabletop surface.

The tabletop itself is constructed from three boards bookmatched at 30 degrees to create a dynamic look. I wanted something a little more modern than a perfectly symmetrical rectangle, and this seemed like a great way to achieve that while still maintaining stability. The legs are all solid walnut, roughly 4" x 4" – I opted for tapered legs to give it a slightly more refined feel, which was a challenge in itself!

Now, let's talk about the inevitable hiccup. During the glue-up of the tabletop, I completely misjudged the fit between two of the boards. I’d meticulously planned everything out, but a slight variation in the wood grain led to a gap that just wouldn’t align properly. Panic set in for a moment! The solution was clear – and humbling - I had to re-plane one of the legs to bring it back up to size. It was a tough pill to swallow, especially after putting so much time into the table, but honestly, it forced me to really examine my techniques and appreciate the importance of precision. It’s amazing how quickly a small mistake can snowball if you don't address it promptly.

The build process took approximately 180 hours – spread over about six months, with periods of intense focus followed by weeks where I just couldn't get to it. But honestly, every hour was worth it.

Once the tabletop was assembled and clamped up, I let it cure for a week before moving on to finishing. I chose a traditional shellac finish, applied in three coats over several days. Shellac gives you an incredible depth of color and a beautiful sheen that really brings out the beauty of the walnut. It’s also incredibly forgiving – unlike some finishes, it can be easily corrected if you make a mistake. I used a satin varnish to protect the shellac layer and add extra durability.

The legs were finished with the same shellac/varnish combination, applied in thin coats with plenty of light sanding between each one. It’s a labor-intensive process, but it's absolutely worth it for the final result. 

I'm incredibly proud of how this table turned out and I really wanted to share my experience – both the triumphs and the challenges – with you all. Building this has been an incredible learning experience, and I feel like a much more confident woodworker because of it.
