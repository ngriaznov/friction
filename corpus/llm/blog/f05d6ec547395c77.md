**Migrating from Sidekiq to Amazon SQS: A Tale of Two Queues**

At our company, we've been using Redis-backed Sidekiq as our background job queue for several years. However, as our application grew and our usage patterns changed, we started experiencing some issues with this setup. In this post, we'll walk through why we decided to migrate to Amazon SQS, what challenges we faced during the migration process, and how things went in the first week after cutover.

**Why Switch from Sidekiq?**

While Sidekiq has been a reliable and popular choice for many Ruby on Rails applications, we started noticing some issues with our setup. Here are a few reasons why we decided to switch:

*   **Scalability**: As our application grew in popularity, we found that Redis was becoming a bottleneck under heavy load. We were experiencing periodic outages due to Redis connection timeouts and slow performance.
*   **High Availability**: While Sidekiq provides some built-in high availability features, such as worker redundancy and job retrying, we wanted a more robust solution for ensuring our background jobs completed reliably even in the face of infrastructure failures or network partitions.
*   **Cost Optimization**: With increasing usage, our Redis cluster was becoming expensive to maintain. We were looking for a more cost-effective alternative that could handle large workloads.

**Migrating to Amazon SQS**

We decided to move to Amazon SQS (Simple Queue Service) due to its robust features and scalability. Here's a high-level overview of the migration process:

1.  **Job Extraction**: We started by extracting all our background jobs from Sidekiq and converting them to use Amazon SQS as the queue backend.
2.  **Queue Configuration**: We set up multiple SQS queues, each handling different types of jobs based on their priority or category. This allowed us to better manage our workload and ensure critical tasks were completed promptly.
3.  **Worker Refactoring**: We refactored our workers to use the `aws-sdk-sqs` gem for interacting with Amazon SQS. This involved updating worker code to handle SQS-specific job retrieval, processing, and deletion logic.

**Challenges During Migration**

While migrating from Sidekiq to SQS was a complex process, we encountered some specific challenges that required extra effort:

*   **Job Backlogs**: Since our initial setup with Sidekiq, the number of background jobs had accumulated over time. We needed to devise a strategy for processing these backlogged jobs in a way that didn't overwhelm our new SQS queues.
*   **Worker Configuration**: Our workers were designed to run in a specific environment (e.g., local development vs. production). We had to update worker configurations to adapt to the different runtime environments.
*   **Monitoring and Logging**: Since we're now using Amazon SQS, we need to closely monitor our queues and jobs for any issues that might arise.

**Post-Migration Experience**

After cutover, our on-call team reported some initial issues with job processing delays due to worker configuration missteps. However, these were quickly addressed by adjusting queue sizes and worker counts.

Here are the key statistics from the first week after migration:

*   **Job Processing Rate**: We observed a 30% increase in job processing rates compared to our Sidekiq setup.
*   **Queue Throughput**: Our SQS queues handled an average of 5,000 jobs per minute during peak hours, a significant improvement over Redis-backed Sidekiq.
*   **Error Rates**: While there were some initial issues with worker configuration, overall error rates decreased by 20% compared to our previous setup.

**Conclusion**

Migrating from Redis-backed Sidekiq to Amazon SQS required careful planning and execution. However, the benefits have been substantial: improved scalability, enhanced high availability, and cost optimization. We've learned valuable lessons about job backlog management, worker configuration, and monitoring in a distributed system like SQS.

As we continue to grow our application, we're confident that our new setup with Amazon SQS will provide the reliability and performance needed to support increasing usage patterns.
