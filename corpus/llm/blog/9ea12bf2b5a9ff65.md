Title: Two Years in Plain JavaScript and My First Impressions of TypeScript

As I sit here reflecting on my journey from plain JavaScript to TypeScript, I am reminded that the journey of learning a new programming language is never straightforward. For two years now, I have been writing code on a mid-sized web app using plain JavaScript. It's been an exhilarating ride filled with moments of excitement and frustration, but it was also a chance for me to develop my skills in a way that felt organic and natural.

When the opportunity arose to explore TypeScript, I jumped at the chance. My first impression was that of a breath of fresh air. The syntax is clean, and the type system feels like a welcome change from the loose, dynamic nature of plain JavaScript. In this post, I'll share my initial thoughts on how TypeScript has impacted my development process and what I've learned so far.

One thing that's struck me about TypeScript is its ability to catch certain types of errors that were once easy to overlook in plain JavaScript. For instance, when using generics with classes or interfaces, TypeScript can now check for type parameters being applied consistently across the codebase. This has saved me from countless hours of debugging and ensured my code is more maintainable.

Another area where TypeScript shines is with static typing. The ability to declare variables as specific types makes it easier to identify potential bugs before they even reach runtime. For example, when working with functions that take parameters, I can now specify the type of those parameters directly in the function declaration. This has helped me catch errors related to mismatched data types or incorrect argument lengths.

On the other hand, some TypeScript features have taken longer for me to adjust to. One area where it feels like busywork is with the use of enums and generics when dealing with simple numeric values. In plain JavaScript, I could simply assign a value to a variable and move on without worrying about its type. However, in TypeScript, those same variables now require explicit type declarations.

Another thing that's taken some time for me to get used to is the concept of interfaces in TypeScript. At first, they seemed like an unnecessary layer of abstraction, but as I continued using them, I realized their importance in defining contracts between modules and ensuring data consistency throughout my codebase.

Despite some initial frustrations with these features, I've come to appreciate how they force me to rethink my coding habits and consider the broader implications of my choices. For instance, when working on a complex system with multiple dependencies, using interfaces has helped me organize my thoughts and ensure that my code is more modular and reusable.

In conclusion, my two years in plain JavaScript have given me a solid foundation for learning TypeScript. While it's not perfect, I've been pleasantly surprised by how well it's integrated into our development workflow. As I continue to explore the language, I'm excited to see where its strengths will take me and what new opportunities it will bring.

Overall, my experience with TypeScript has taught me that adopting a new programming language is never just about mastering the syntax; it's also about understanding how it will change the way you approach problem-solving. With that in mind, I'm eager to continue learning and exploring the possibilities of this powerful tool.
